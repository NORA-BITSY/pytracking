1. Authoritative Guidelines & Consensus Documents

Source Why it matters Where to get it
Society of Hair Testing (SoHT) Consensus Documents De-facto international standards for cut-offs, collection, decontamination, and result interpretation. Free PDFs on the SoHT site → "Consensus on Hair Testing (Rev 2022)" and "Consensus on Drugs of Abuse Testing in Hair (Rev 2021)" (soht.org)
SAMHSA Mandatory Guidelines (proposed) Set the rules for U.S. federal workplace programs; contain tables of analytes & decision cut-offs. 2020 Federal Register notice + 2023 update deck (PDF) (SAMHSA)
NIJ / DOJ Technical Bulletins U.S. criminal-justice research on external contamination vs. true ingestion. NIJ article "Detecting Drugs in Hair: Is It Drug Use or Environmental Contamination?" (National Institute of Justice)

2. Peer-Reviewed Data Sets & Validation Studies

  • PubMed / NCBI – Search terms like
    • "hair toxicology" AND cocaine AND cutoff
    • "external contamination" hair metabolite ratio
    The 2014 NIDA study on concordance between self-report and hair positives is a good example. (PMC)
  • Open-access repositories – Many SoHT round-robin/proficiency datasets get archived in journals such as Forensic Science International (often green-open on PubMed Central).
  • ResearchGate – Authors often upload the full SoHT guideline PDFs and validation charts even when pay-walled elsewhere. (ResearchGate)

3. Government & Regulatory Dockets

Agency What you'll find How
SAMHSA / HHS Slide decks & comment summaries for hair testing rule-making (metabolite tables, LOQs, inter-lab precision). Search site:samhsa.gov hair testing pdf. The "Drug Metabolites and Hair Testing" primer is a good starting doc. (SAMHSA)
Federal Register Full text of the proposed Mandatory Guidelines; public comments often include lab error-rate data. Search the docket number 2020-16432 or Docket: SAMHSA-2025-0001.
NIJ / OJP Grants & results on contamination, racial bias, and physicochemical binding to melanin (useful if you're litigating). Use NIJ's site search.

4. Proficiency-Testing & Round-Robin Data

  • SoHT Collaborative Exercises – Annual blind-sample programs; summary statistical reports show inter-laboratory false-positive/negative rates.
  • RTI International / CAP Forensic Drug Testing PT – U.S. proficiency programs (you can FOIA aggregate stats from federal grantees).
  • NIST – Occasionally releases Standard Reference Materials (SRMs) for hair matrices; check the SRM catalog.

5. Case-Law & Reliability Challenges

If you need precedent on admissibility or bias:

Resource Example
Lexis / Westlaw Hogan v. City of Boston hair-test discrimination case (cocaine / melanin) – provides expert affidavits on error rates. (WIRED)
Google Scholar (Case law tab) Search hair testing Frye or Daubert hair follicle.
NIJ / DOJ Library White papers summarizing courtroom challenges to hair evidence.

6. Practical Tips for Getting Raw Lab Data

  1. Discovery / Subpoena – Accredited labs keep chromatograms & QC charts for at least 2 years. Ask for:
    • Chain-of-custody logs
    • Wash protocols & residual analyte levels
    • Parent-to-metabolite ratios (distinguishes ingestion from contamination)
  2. Freedom of Information Act (FOIA) – For federally funded labs or agencies.
  3. Ask for "Laboratory Developed Test" (LDT) validation packets – CLIA-certified labs must show accuracy, precision, linearity.

Quick Search Workflow

  1. Start broad on PubMed → filter to last 5 years.
  2. Grab the guideline PDFs (SoHT + SAMHSA) for baseline numbers.
  3. Cross-check any surprising cut-off or error-rate figure against SoHT round-robin reports.
  4. Use case-law databases to see how courts have weighed the science in admissibility hearings.
  5. If you still can't locate a specific dataset, request it from the author's correspondence email—hair-testing researchers are usually responsive.
